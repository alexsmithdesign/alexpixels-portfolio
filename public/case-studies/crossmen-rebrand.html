Crossmen

BRANDING, WEB DESIGN, LOGO

The Crossmen Drum & Bugle Corps has shaped young performers since 1974. A corps that once placed 6th at DCI World Championships is now finishing outside the top 15, operating on razor-thin margins, and asking for more from an alumni base that's losing confidence. This case study is a speculative rebrand exploring what it looks like when the Crossmen's visual identity finally matches the World Class quality they’re capable of.

THE OLD LOGOS

The original Maltese cross logo was legible, bold, directly tied to the name, but it was generic, indistinct, and had a long history predating the Crossmen. It's a deeply historical symbol passed off as logo design, and without distinctive construction or unique proportions, it could belong to a hundred other organizations.

The "C" they replaced it with solved one problem and created another. The execution was too safe. A generic "C" with no distinctive shapes, no personality, and no connection to the corps' character is just typography masquerading as a logo.

The deeper issue with both marks is that neither one communicates anything about the corps. The Crossmen are a high-performance youth performing arts organization with an hard working culture and 50 years of identity. The brand had no visual language for any of that.

THE NEW LOGO

The new logo introduces a callback to the old cross, and features spikes radiating outward to convey the explosive movement, sound, and energy Crossmen are known for. This resonates with the Crossmen's history while adding a distinctive flair associated with their own identity. An elaborate cross harkens to a time when these kinds of forms and shapes were adorned amongst cathedrals and structures. Much like the complexity and unpredictability of a cathedral window, the symbol captures the deep and cerebral jazz they're known for. The blackletter lettering was crafted to tie in with the cross and it's historical vibe.

THE OLD WEBSITE

On the old site, pictured below, there were no distinct visuals. Mostly a lot of loud color blocks and visuals all competing at the same volume. There is a lack of hierarchy to guide the user around the page and to let them know what to look at first and why. The typography usage is inconsitent and has no connection the brand in the way it's presented. The grid lacks intentionality with objects and text causing the eye to bounce around eratically due to excessive center alignment-which making it harder for users to read. Some objects are bright red and clickable, while others are not. This creates confusion on where to click and when.

THE NEW WEBSITE

This new site, which can be viewed by clicking below, addresses many of the concerns mentioned above. The site was designed in Figma, and built using HTML, CSS, and Claude Code. The site was designed in a modular fashion to make it easier to hand off to an organization. A dramatic hero with a video draws users in with an initial strong impression. Navigating the old site and finding the most important items were not very clear and hidden behind multiple menus. The new homepage acts as multiple doorways organized to fewer, more condensed pages.
