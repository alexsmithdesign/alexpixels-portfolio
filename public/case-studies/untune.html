Untune

BRAND IDENTITY, APP DESIGN, WEB DESIGN, LOGO DESIGN

Streaming gave everyone access to everything and ownership of nothing. Albums disappear overnight. Algorithms decide what plays next. Every listen is logged. Untune is a local music player — inspired by the golden age of the desktop library, rebuilt with attitude for people who never stopped collecting. The brand needed to match the product's position: unapologetically anti-streaming, rooted in the physicality of owned music, and sharp enough to mean it.

The Mark

The Untune logo takes the spiral energy of a disc in motion and distills it into an abstract orbital mark — a loop that reads as both a CD and a signal, caught mid-play. The wordmark pairs an industrial oblique display face with the mark's kinetic energy. The palette is severe: near-black, white, and a single hot red-orange that only appears when something is active, live, or demanding attention. Nothing decorates. Everything signals.

Streaming Is In The Past

The visual language draws from The Designers Republic, Y2K industrial print, and the physical artifacts of CD culture — barcodes, registration marks, caution stickers, terminal metadata readouts. Grain and scanline textures reinforce the lo-fi materiality of owned media. The orange neural texture used as a side panel element references both circuitry and the organic chaos of a record collection. This is a brand that wears its influences as badges, not apologies.

The App

The Untune app translates the poster's energy into a functional daily interface. The discipline: the chrome stays punk, the content stays legible. Track lists render as terminal metadata readouts. The now-playing screen is the one place the brand goes full showpiece — lo-fi disc art, scanline treatment, the orbital mark as the live play indicator. The hot orange appears only on active states: the playing row, the progress bar, the live indicator. Everything else is grayscale. The restraint makes the signal impossible to miss.

The Site

The Untune website is a manifesto before it's a product page. It opens with the thesis — streaming is in the past — and earns it before showing a single screenshot. The site argues for music ownership as a position, not a feature. Terminal-style microcopy runs throughout. The download CTA closes with the same attitude the brand opens with. One ask. No catch.
